# Substance: how to say something worth reading

This is the intelligence layer. The rest of the skill removes what should not be there: process bleed, AI tells, fabricated proof, flattened voice. This file is about what should be there instead.

The two jobs are different. `ai-tells.md` tells you what to **cut**. This file tells you what to **put in the gap**, and how to judge whether what you put there is worth anything. Cutting a tell and leaving clean emptiness is only half the work.

## The failure this prevents: clean nothing

A piece can pass every other check in this skill and still be worthless. No em dashes. No "in this guide." No invented metrics. True to voice. And it says nothing a reader could not have guessed before opening it.

That is **clean nothing**: grammatically perfect copy with no information in it. It is the most common result of naive de-slopping, and it is a failure, not a pass. The reader's time is the price. Copy that costs a minute and returns nothing is a bad trade, however clean.

The standard is higher than "reads like a human wrote it." The standard is "a sharp human would be glad they wrote it, and a busy reader is glad they read it."

## The governing rule: insight is compression, not invention

Everything below has one hard boundary. **Value comes from the true material, never from reaching outside it.**

You make copy valuable by finding what is already latent in the source and making it land: the implication the brief did not spell out, the connection between two facts the writer left unconnected, the precise word for the vague feeling, the real question under the stated one. That is intelligence.

Fabricating an impressive claim is the opposite of intelligence. It is the laziest possible move, and this skill forbids it. When the substance is genuinely missing, you do not invent it. You flag it (`[ADD THE REAL REASON THIS WORKS]`, `[NAME THE TRADEOFF]`) and say so outside the copy. A truthful gap beats a confident lie every time.

So: sharpen, compress, connect, reframe. Never manufacture.

## The tests

Apply these to copy that is already clean but feels weightless. Each one is a question with a fix.

### 1. The swap test (does only you say this?)

Could this exact sentence appear, unchanged, in a competitor's copy, or on a completely different product? If yes, it carries no information.

The fix is not more adjectives. It is the specific true thing that is only true here: the actual mechanism, the actual number, the actual tradeoff, the actual user.

- Before: "Our software helps businesses streamline operations and drive better outcomes."
- After: "Close your books in three days, not ten. It pulls every transaction from your bank feed and flags the ones that don't reconcile, so your controller reviews exceptions instead of re-keying receipts."

The number and the mechanism come from the brief. If the brief did not give you "three days," you write `[ADD REAL CLOSE TIME]`, not a guess.

### 2. The negation test (could anyone take the other side?)

A claim is empty if its opposite is absurd. Nobody advertises a commitment to low quality, so "committed to quality" says nothing. The sentence has no edge because no one stands on the other side of it.

A claim is real if a reasonable competitor might genuinely refuse to make it.

- Empty: "We put our customers first."
- Real: "We answer support tickets in under an hour, nights and weekends included. Miss that window and your next month is free."

A competitor might honestly decline that promise. It has a cost. That cost is what makes it worth saying. (And the specifics are briefed, or they are placeholders.)

### 3. Earn the claim (show the mechanism)

A claim the reader has to take on faith is worth little, however bold. The valuable version shows the mechanism, so the reader sees why it is true and could check it. Demonstrated beats declared. This is the line between copy that only persuades and writing that actually advances what the reader knows.

- Asserted: "Our approach dramatically improves retention."
- Earned: "Retention improves because the first email arrives while the user is still in the product, not the next morning. People finish setup when the next step is in front of them, not buried in an inbox."

The earned version makes the same claim and hands the reader the reasoning. They can agree, disagree, or test it. That is what makes words valuable rather than merely convincing: the reader leaves understanding something, not just believing you. Take the mechanism from the true material. If you do not know why it works, say so and find out. Do not dress an assertion up as a reason.

### 4. The so-what ladder (compress a fact to its consequence)

A bare fact is rarely the point. The point is what the fact does for the reader. Find it by asking "so what?" until the answer is something the reader can act on or feel.

- Fact: "The dashboard updates in real time."
- So what? You see the number move as it happens.
- So what? You stop refreshing a spreadsheet to find out if the campaign is working.
- Landing: "You'll know the campaign is failing by lunch, not in Monday's report, while there's still time to fix it."

Same fact, no addition. The value was always inside it. You compressed down to the consequence instead of stopping at the feature.

### 5. The real question (answer the one under the one)

Weak copy answers the stated question. Strong copy answers the real one underneath it, which is usually about risk, status, or effort.

- Stated: "Is your platform secure?"
- Real: "If our data leaks, am I the one who gets fired?"
- Weak answer: "We use bank-level encryption and follow industry best practices."
- Strong answer: "Every access is logged with a name and a timestamp. When your auditor asks who touched a record, you have the answer on one screen, not a support ticket."

The strong answer addresses the fear, not the FAQ. Feeling understood is the actual mechanism of trust, and it is the thing generic copy can never fake.

### 6. Keep the tension (stakes, limits, who it's not for)

Slop is frictionless. Everything is great, nothing is hard, no choice has a cost. That is exactly why it is forgettable. Real writing names the hard part: the tradeoff, the limit, the person this is not for.

- Slop: "Perfect for teams of all sizes."
- Real: "Built for teams of five to fifty. Below five, a shared inbox is cheaper and works fine. Above fifty, you'll want role-based permissions we don't offer yet."

Naming who it is not for is one of the strongest trust signals in copy, and slop never does it. You are not inventing a flaw. You are being honest about the real boundary, which makes every other claim more believable.

### 7. Density (one exact word beats three vague ones)

Vagueness is unfinished thinking. When a sentence sprawls, the thought underneath is not done yet. Intelligence reads as effortless because the work happened before the words: the writer found the exact distinction, so the sentence got short.

- Vague: "Our onboarding is designed to be as smooth and frictionless as possible so users can get up and running quickly and efficiently."
- Dense: "You send your first real email twelve minutes after signup."

The fix for a sprawling sentence is not to polish it. It is to figure out what you actually mean, then say only that.

### 8. Cliché is a placeholder for a thought you haven't had yet

"At the end of the day," "move the needle," "best-in-class," "take it to the next level." Each one marks a spot where the writer reached for the nearest familiar phrase instead of the true specific. Removing the cliché is easy. The work is replacing it with what you actually meant.

- Cliché: "At the end of the day, we're committed to moving the needle for our clients."
- Underneath: "Most agencies bill for hours. We bill for booked meetings. If your calendar doesn't fill, neither does our invoice."

Ask of every cliché: what was I trying to say before I reached for that phrase? Say that.

## By mode

**Drafting.** You have the most room here, but the room is inside the brief. Bring the sharpest true framing the material supports. Lead with the reader's real question. Make claims that pass the negation test. Where the brief lacks the specific that would make a line land, place a visible placeholder rather than a confident guess.

**Cleanup.** The harder case. If the draft is clean but empty, you cannot inject substance the source does not support, because that is invention. You do two things: compress and sharpen what is genuinely there, and surface the emptiness honestly. A section that makes no claim a reader could disagree with does not need polish; it needs a real point or real proof. Say so outside the copy and mark the gap. Do not buff clean nothing into shinier nothing.

## The voice guard

Substance sharpens the user's point. It does not replace it with yours.

When making a line land would change what the user is actually claiming, their position, their argument, their stance, stop. Flag it and ask, or note it outside the copy. The goal is to make their thinking land harder, not to swap in your thinking because it makes a cleaner sentence. A brilliant line that argues something the user did not mean is a failure, not a save.

## What valuable looks like

Before shipping, the strongest lines in the piece should do at least one of these:

- Make the reader think "I hadn't seen it that way," "that's exactly right," or "now I know what to do."
- Say something only this writer, about this thing, could say.
- Make a claim a real competitor might refuse to make, and back it.
- Show why a claim is true, so the reader could check it, instead of asking them to take it on faith.
- Name the hard part instead of pretending there isn't one.
- Compress a known fact down to the consequence the reader actually cares about.

If no line in a section does any of these, the section is clean nothing. Fix it or flag it. Do not ship it as if clean were the same as worth reading.
