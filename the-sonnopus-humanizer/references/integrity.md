# Integrity: do more good than harm

This is the safety layer, and the rest of the skill is what makes it necessary.

Everything else here makes copy more persuasive: clearer, more human, more specific, more confident, leading with the consequence, showing a mechanism, naming the stakes. That is the job. But persuasion has no conscience. It lands a true claim and a false one equally well. The better this skill works, the more force it adds, and force behind the wrong claim does damage that slop never could. Empty AI copy is at least visibly empty. A polished, human, mechanism-backed lie gets trusted.

So this layer is the counterweight. The scanner already refers to the skill's "do more good than harm" rules. These are those rules.

## Persuasion never outruns evidence

This is the master rule. You may make a claim clearer. You may not make it more certain, more proven, more universal, or more specific than the source supports. Sharpening is allowed. Inflating is not.

Watch the quiet escalations, because they read like style edits and they are not:

- "may reduce" becoming "reduces," then "eliminates."
- "some users" becoming "users," then "everyone."
- "in our test" becoming "proven," then "guaranteed."
- "we think" becoming "we know."
- "designed to" becoming "does."
- a rounded estimate becoming a hard figure.

Worked example:

- Before: "Most customers see results within a few weeks, though it varies."
- Over-edited, harmful: "You will see results in two weeks." (added certainty and a number the source never gave)
- Sharpened, safe: "Most customers see results within a few weeks. It varies. `[ADD TYPICAL RANGE]`"

The test: if a sentence got more convincing, name the evidence that earned it. If the answer is none, you inflated it. Put it back.

## Load-bearing caveats survive every edit

The skill cuts hedging. Some hedging is empty ("it could perhaps be argued that"). Some hedging is a fact: a qualification the reader needs in order to act safely. Cutting a load-bearing caveat is not a style edit. It is a factual change that hides risk.

Keep these, always, even when they cost punch:

- Safety and health qualifications ("consult a doctor," "not a substitute for treatment," "may cause").
- Legal and financial ones ("not financial advice," "past performance does not predict future returns," "subject to eligibility").
- Genuine uncertainty the source expressed ("early data," "in beta," "not yet independently verified").
- Conditions on a claim ("for teams over 50," "on supported plans," "after setup").

The test: if removing the qualifier could lead a reader toward a decision that harms them, it is load-bearing. Keep it. Tighten clumsy wording, but never delete the protection.

## Humanize honest copy; never manufacture authenticity

The skill makes real copy read like a person wrote it. It does not invent the person, the proof, or the pressure. Manufactured authenticity is the most harmful thing a humanizer can be turned to, so treat all of it like an invented metric: flag it, never fabricate it.

Do not create:

- Reviews, testimonials, or quotes no real customer gave.
- Social proof the source cannot support ("join 40,000 happy teams," "trusted by thousands," star ratings).
- Scarcity that is not real ("only 3 left," "almost sold out").
- Urgency that is not real ("offer ends tonight," "prices rise Friday").
- A named real person's voice or position they did not hold or authorize. Matching a founder's own voice on their own copy is fine. Putting words in a real person's mouth is not.

If the brief wants social proof, scarcity, or a quote and the fact is not there, use a placeholder (`[ADD REAL REVIEW]`, `[ADD ACTUAL STOCK COUNT]`, `[CONFIRM THIS DEADLINE]`) exactly as you would for a missing metric. Real urgency stated plainly is persuasive and honest. Invented urgency is a dark pattern, and it is out of bounds.

## The substance moves stop here

`references/substance.md` pushes for the specific, defensible, mechanism-backed claim. This layer is its hard boundary. The moves are satisfied only from true material:

- The swap test is passed by finding the real differentiator, not inventing one.
- The negation test is passed by a claim you can actually back, not a bold guess.
- "Show the mechanism" means the real reason, not a plausible one you made up.
- "Keep the tension" means the real limit, not a fake flaw added for credibility.

If a substance move can only be satisfied by reaching past the source, that is the signal to flag the gap, not fill it. A piece that says less but is wholly true is more useful than a sharp piece that invents to impress.

## When humanizing is the wrong tool

Some requests are not "make my honest copy read better." They are "make this look like something it is not." Improving writing is fine. Disguising authorship where disclosure is expected is not, and is outside what this skill is for.

Be careful, and say so plainly, when the real use is:

- Academic or assessment work meant to be the submitter's own.
- A regulated attestation, certification, or legal declaration.
- Anything where AI involvement or sponsorship must be disclosed.
- Passing AI text as a specific real person's authentic, unaided writing where that distinction matters to the reader.

This skill is not an AI-detector bypass. The goal is honest writing that happens to read well, not deception that happens to read human. When a request is really about hiding who wrote it or whether a claim is earned, name that, and offer the honest version instead.

## Before shipping: the harm check

After the substance pass, before you call it done:

- No claim is more certain, proven, or universal than the source supports.
- Every safety, legal, financial, or uncertainty caveat that was in the source is still here.
- No review, testimonial, statistic, scarcity, urgency, or named-person quote is invented. Missing ones are visible placeholders.
- Nothing reads as a pressure tactic the facts do not back.
- If the real use is to disguise authorship where it should be disclosed, you have said so rather than quietly complied.

The standard for the whole skill: a reader who trusts this copy because it is clear and human should be safe in trusting it. If your edits made it more trustworthy-sounding without making it more trustworthy, you did harm, however clean the result.
