# Process bleed reference

Use this file when copy still sounds like it came from a chat, outline, roadmap, markdown scaffold, or internal planning session.

The fix is always the same: stop describing the making, describe the thing.

## The sentence test

Would this sentence make sense to a reader who never saw the project, chat, plan, outline, prompt, or drafting process?

If not, rewrite it around the reader, product, offer, proof, objection, or next step.

## Fast scan phrases

These phrases often introduce process bleed. Confirm with the sentence test before cutting.

Roadmap narration: "we started by," "first we," "the first step was," "this leads us to," "moving into the next phase," "building on that," "now we turn to," "finally we arrive at."

Artifact meta: "in this article," "in this guide," "in this section," "this report outlines," "the purpose of this document," "now that we've covered," "as established earlier," "as we'll see."

Conversation residue: "as you mentioned," "per your request," "based on our discussion," "as requested," "you asked me to," "based on our previous conversation."

Iteration disclosure: "after careful consideration," "after several drafts," "what we settled on," "what emerged was," "we landed on," "we decided," "we considered."

Effort padding and self-praise: "through extensive research," "carefully crafted," "our commitment to," "comprehensive guide," "definitive resource," "best-in-class content."

Delivery wrapper: "here is the copy," "below is the draft," "I hope this helps," "let me know if you'd like."

## Seven leak types

### 1. Roadmap narration

Before: In this guide, we'll start with the fundamentals, then build toward advanced tactics, and finally bring it all together.

After: Most teams get the fundamentals wrong in the same three places. Fix those first, and the advanced tactics start working on their own.

Why: The reader does not care what order the piece was written in. Open on the problem or point.

### 2. Artifact meta

Before: This section explores why retention matters for subscription businesses.

After: Retention decides whether a subscription business compounds or leaks revenue every month.

Why: Say the thing. Do not announce that the thing is about to be said.

### 3. Conversation residue

Before: Based on our previous discussion, the homepage should focus on speed, trust, and simplicity.

After: The homepage has three jobs: prove the product is fast, make it feel safe, and show that setup is simple.

Why: The reader did not see the conversation.

### 4. Decision disclosure

Before: After considering several positioning options, we landed on compliance-first messaging.

After: Compliance should lead the message because it is the buyer's first risk question.

Why: Give the conclusion, not the internal decision path.

### 5. Effort padding

Before: Through extensive research and countless hours of iteration, our team built a comprehensive resource for retirement planning.

After: Retirement math breaks down in a few predictable spots. Here is where, and what to do about each one.

Why: Effort spent making the thing is not the reader benefit.

### 6. Scaffold residue

Before: Phase 1: Awareness. Phase 2: Consideration. [expand with stats later]

After: A buyer usually arrives with one question: will this work for a company like mine? The page needs proof before persuasion. [ADD REAL CUSTOMER PROOF]

Why: Convert planning labels into reader-facing meaning. Keep factual placeholders visible when proof is missing.

### 7. Self-praise

Before: This definitive, carefully crafted guide gives you everything you need to succeed.

After: Cut it, or replace it with a concrete benefit the guide actually delivers.

Why: Let the usefulness of the copy earn trust.

## Boundary cases

Changelogs, release notes, migration guides, and project retrospectives may legitimately discuss what changed or how something was built. That is not process bleed if the reader came for that information.

A genuine cross-reference in a long report can be useful. A constant habit of "as discussed earlier" usually means the document is narrating itself.

An email greeting or sign-off belongs in an email. A greeting or sign-off pasted into website copy is wrapper residue.
